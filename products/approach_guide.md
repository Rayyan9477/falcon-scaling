# The Family Office Approach Playbook
## A Practitioner's Guide to Co-Investment with Family Offices

---

## Chapter 1: How Family Offices Make Co-Investment Decisions

### The Decision Architecture
Family office co-investment decisions follow a fundamentally different process than institutional LP commitments. Understanding this is the difference between getting a meeting and getting ignored.

**The 3-Gate Model:**
1. **Gate 1 — Trust Filter** (Eliminates 80% of inbound): Does the FO know you, or someone who knows you? Cold outreach has a <5% response rate with FOs. Warm introductions pass this gate.

2. **Gate 2 — Thesis Alignment** (Eliminates 50% of remaining): Does your deal/fund match their investment thesis? FOs are thesis-driven, not return-driven. A 3x healthcare deal is irrelevant to an FO focused on real estate.

3. **Gate 3 — Principal Conviction** (Final filter): Does the principal (family member or CIO) personally believe in this? FOs don't have investment committees in the institutional sense. One person's conviction often drives the decision.

### SFO vs. MFO Decision Dynamics
- **Single Family Offices**: Decisions are faster (days to weeks), but harder to access. One person often has final say. Relationships are personal — if the principal likes you, deals happen. If they don't, no amount of returns data matters.

- **Multi-Family Offices**: More structured process (similar to institutional), multiple stakeholders, formal due diligence. Slower but more predictable. Access is through the professional staff, not the families directly.

### What FOs Care About (That Most Fund Managers Miss)
1. **Alignment of interest**: Do you have significant personal capital in the deal?
2. **Information flow**: Will they get regular, candid updates? Not quarterly reports — real conversations.
3. **Co-investment rights**: Can they participate in follow-on rounds or adjacent deals?
4. **Reputation risk**: Will this deal embarrass the family? FOs are ultra-conservative about public perception.
5. **Exit timeline**: Most FOs think in decades, not fund cycles. Multi-generational time horizons.

## Chapter 2: The 5 Types of FO Co-Investors

### Type 1: The Active Allocator (Co-Invest Score 8-10)
**Profile**: Dedicated investment team, regular deal flow review, systematic process.
**Examples**: Iconiq Capital, Bessemer Trust, Cascade Investment
**How to approach**: Formal pitch process through their deal intake. They expect institutional-quality materials. Lead with data, not relationships.
**What to send**: 1-page teaser, then full deck upon request. Include detailed financials.

### Type 2: The Opportunistic Principal (Co-Invest Score 6-8)
**Profile**: Family principal who does select direct deals based on personal interest/expertise.
**Examples**: Thiel Capital, Schmidt Family Office, Pritzker Group
**How to approach**: Through mutual connections. The principal must personally connect with the opportunity. Lead with the story, not the numbers.
**What to send**: Short narrative email (3 paragraphs max), then a conversation, then materials.

### Type 3: The Fund-of-Funds LP (Co-Invest Score 4-6)
**Profile**: Primarily invests through funds, occasionally does co-investments alongside trusted GPs.
**Examples**: Many European and Asian FOs in this category.
**How to approach**: Through their existing GP relationships. If you're not already in their fund portfolio, co-investment is unlikely unless introduced by a GP they trust.
**What to send**: Introduction via mutual GP. "We're doing a co-invest alongside [GP they know]."

### Type 4: The Sector Specialist (Co-Invest Score 5-8)
**Profile**: Deep expertise in one sector, only co-invests in that vertical.
**Examples**: Poonawalla (healthcare), Grok Ventures (deep tech), Koch (energy/industrials)
**How to approach**: Through sector-specific conferences and networks. Lead with domain expertise — they'll test your knowledge. If you can't discuss the sector at their level, you won't pass Gate 1.
**What to send**: Sector thesis first, deal second. "Here's how we see the [sector] landscape evolving."

### Type 5: The Legacy Holder (Co-Invest Score 1-3)
**Profile**: Primarily manages existing family assets, minimal new investment activity.
**Examples**: Cadogan Estate, Portman Family Office, many 3rd+ generation offices
**How to approach**: Generally not worth pursuing for co-investment. Their mandate is preservation, not growth. If you do approach, frame as ultra-low-risk, income-generating, or asset-backed.
**What to send**: Don't. Unless you have a warm intro and a property/infrastructure deal.

| Caproasia Summit | Southeast Asian FOs |

### Conference Playbook
1. **Pre-event** (2-4 weeks before): Research which FOs are attending (speaker lists, sponsor lists). Send targeted LinkedIn connection requests. "Looking forward to connecting at [Event]."
2. **At the event**: Focus on 5-7 target FOs. Don't pitch in the first conversation — build rapport. Ask about their investment thesis, not your deal.
3. **Post-event** (within 48 hours): Send personalized follow-up referencing your conversation. "Great discussing [specific topic] — I'd love to continue that conversation."
4. **Follow-through** (2-4 weeks later): Share something valuable (research, deal insight, introduction) before asking for anything.

## Chapter 5: Red Flags That Get You Blacklisted

1. **Mass emails**: FO decision makers can spot batch outreach instantly. One generic email destroys your reputation permanently.
2. **Name-dropping without permission**: "I was talking to [other FO principal]..." Unless they explicitly authorized you to mention them, don't.
3. **Inflated track record**: FOs do deep reference checks. If your 3x return was really 1.5x after fees, they'll find out.
4. **Pushing for a meeting**: "Just following up for the 4th time..." Two follow-ups maximum. If they haven't responded, they're not interested.
5. **Sharing confidential deal info**: If you share another FO's deal data to impress them, they'll assume you'll share theirs too.
6. **Misunderstanding family dynamics**: Approaching the wrong family member, or not understanding internal family tensions. Research the family structure first.
7. **Treating FOs like institutional LPs**: Sending 60-page DDQs, requesting formal meetings with "the investment committee." FOs don't work like pension funds.

## Chapter 6: Building Long-Term FO Relationships

### The 12-Month Relationship Framework

**Months 1-3: Establish Presence**
- Connect on LinkedIn
- Share 2-3 pieces of genuinely useful sector research (not your marketing materials)
- Attend one event where they'll be present

**Months 4-6: Add Value First**
- Make an introduction that benefits THEM (not you)
- Share a deal they might be interested in (even if it's not yours)
- Comment thoughtfully on their LinkedIn posts or press mentions

**Months 7-9: Deepen the Relationship**
- Request a casual coffee/call — "No agenda, just want to understand your current thinking on [sector]"
- Share proprietary market insights from your deal flow
- If appropriate, invite them to a private event you're hosting

**Months 10-12: Present an Opportunity**
- By now they know you, trust you, and understand your expertise
- Present your deal/fund as a natural extension of the relationship
- Frame it as: "Based on our conversations about [sector], I think this might align with what you're looking for"

### The Cardinal Rule
**Give before you ask. Always.**

The fund managers who succeed with family offices are the ones who spend 12 months building a relationship before ever mentioning a deal. The ones who fail are the ones who pitch on first contact.

Family office capital is patient capital. Your approach should be equally patient.

## Important: Regulatory Considerations

This guide provides relationship and networking strategy. It does not constitute legal or compliance advice. Fund managers and placement agents should be aware of:

- **US**: Regulation D (Rule 506(b) and 506(c)) governs private placement solicitation. General solicitation rules apply. Placement agents may need SEC or state registration.
- **Europe**: AIFMD marketing passport rules restrict how non-EU managers can approach EU-based FOs. Each jurisdiction has specific requirements.
- **Asia**: MAS (Singapore), SFC (Hong Kong), and SEBI (India) each have distinct rules around approaching institutional/accredited investors.
- **Placement agents**: May require FINRA registration (US), FCA authorization (UK), or equivalent local licensing.

**Always consult qualified legal counsel** before initiating any fundraising outreach. Regulatory requirements vary by jurisdiction, fund structure, and investor type. Family offices may qualify as "accredited investors" or "qualified purchasers" but this must be verified on a case-by-case basis.

Key industry networks for staying current on FO regulatory frameworks:
- **Family Office Exchange (FOX)** — the most established FO peer network globally
- **Tiger 21** — peer-to-peer network for UHNW investors and family offices
- **CAIA Association** — credentialing body for alternative investment professionals
